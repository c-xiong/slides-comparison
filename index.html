Background
Two distinct leakage concerns
Source: Kapoor and Narayanan - 2023 - Leakage and the reproducibility crisis in machine-learning-based science
L1.2 — Preprocessing leakage

If LDA is fit on the full 1982–2025 corpus, the topic representation of an article from, say, 2005 is informed by term-count statistics that include 2010–2025 text.

"θ̂_t is informed in part by term counts that realize after time t." Bybee et al. - 2024 - Business News and Business Cycles (p. 3114)
L1.3 — Hyperparameter selection leakage

If K is chosen by minimizing OOS forecast RMSE on a grid, then K itself is a function of the test set. This is unambiguous test-set contamination.

Ver1 had both L1.2 and L1.3.
Ver2 fixed both but introduced multi-checkpoint reestimation with no clear precedent in this literature.

Method A — Single-Checkpoint Frozen LDA
Three top-field papers; acknowledged staleness cost
"the news corpus is truncated to end on the last day of 2003, and 80 news topics are estimated using the LDA model. Then, using the estimated topic distributions from the truncated news corpus, the corpus for the period 2004 to 2014 is classified… the sampler is run with the estimated word distributions (from the training sample) held constant." Larsen and Thorsrud - 2019 - The value of news for economic developments (p. 209)
"to avoid potential look-ahead biases, the LDA model was estimated on the initial training sample only, that is, all the news after 1995Q4 is classified OOS using the topic distributions learned from the 1985Q1 to 1995Q4 sample." Ellingsen et al. - 2022 - News media versus FRED-MD for macroeconomic forecasting (Section 3 footnote)
"the average relative performance seems to deteriorate somewhat towards the end of the evaluation sample. One potential reason for this might be that the estimated topic distributions are not recursively updated when doing the out-of-sample experiment. At the end of the out-of-sample evaluation period we are in essence classifying the news corpus with 10-year-old topic estimates." Larsen and Thorsrud - 2019 - The value of news for economic developments (p. 210) — acknowledged cost

Our setting: With a 1998-01 cutoff, by 2025 we classify with ~27-year-old topic estimates. Post-1998 themes (COVID, ESG, crypto, AI) have no native topic and are projected onto nearest 1998-era topic in word distribution. We address this empirically by reporting Method A as a robustness check, not the main spec.

Method B — Online LDA (oLDA)
Four reasons we do not adopt oLDA
  • 1 Engineering cost. Our pipeline uses scikit-learn's LatentDirichletAllocation in a batch-then-freeze pattern. True oLDA forecasting requires interleaving .transform() and .partial_fit() inside the OOS loop, with hyperparameters (learning rate κ, decay τ₀, batch ordering by time) tuned to ensure convergence. This is a substantial re-engineering, not a parameter change.
  • 2 Burn-in cost. Bybee et al. (2024, p. 3141) drop the first 4 years of their sample for oLDA burn-in. Our OOS window already starts 2014-01 due to the 192-month initial training requirement; adding a 4-year burn-in further shortens an already COVID-dominated window.
  • 3 Marginal benefit is reported as small (Bybee et al. 2024, p. 3132): "the differences are quantitatively minor. From a parameter uncertainty perspective, at short horizons LDA and oLDA estimates are distinct, but at longer horizons they are statistically indistinguishable."
  • 4 Defensible cheaper alternative exists. Method A captures the leakage-fix benefit at much lower engineering cost and has stronger precedent in this specific literature — three top-field papers vs one.
Method C — Full-Sample LDA (Recommended Main Spec)
Full-sample LDA leakage: quantitatively minor in three studies, accepted without check in a Hong et al.

Empirical robustness checks (bias is quantitatively small)

"when estimating the LDA using the full sample of news data, and then simply truncating the resulting news topics time series for the OOS forecasting experiment, we observe that the results from Section 4.1 remain qualitatively robust (Figure SD7). As also commented on in Larsen and Thorsrud (2019), this suggests that the issue related to look-ahead biases likely is not very empirically important." Ellingsen et al. - 2022 - News media versus FRED-MD for macroeconomic forecasting (Figure SD7)
"the differences [between full-sample LDA and oLDA] are quantitatively minor. From a parameter uncertainty perspective, at short horizons LDA and oLDA estimates are distinct, but at longer horizons they are statistically indistinguishable." Bybee et al. - 2024 - Business News and Business Cycles (Figure 6 / Section IV.C, p. 3132)
Larsen and Thorsrud - 2019 - The value of news for economic developments (p. 211)
Full-sample experiment delivers results consistent with the truncated-training experiment; explicitly noted by Ellingsen et al. (2022) as supporting evidence.

Publication precedent (top-field acceptance without the check)

Hong et al. - 2025 - Forecasting Inflation Using Economic Narratives
Accepted at JBES with no leakage-fix robustness check reported. Sets the floor for this literature: referees did not require the check for publication.
Method D — Multi-Checkpoint Topic Re-Estimation(Our Ver2)
Real-time feasible, but not a clean robustness design
"Unlike rolling reestimation, which faces the issue that topics in one rolling sample are delinked from topics in a prior sample, oLDA preserves a coherent topic interpretation over time." Bybee et al. (2024), Business News and Business Cycles
"Ideally, one might have preferred also updating the topic model recursively. However, recursively re-estimating the topic model is a very computationally demanding task…" Larsen and Thorsrud (2019), The Value of News for Economic Developments

What Method D does

  • Estimate several frozen topic models at different checkpoints, e.g. 1998, 2005, 2012, and 2019.
  • Each article is encoded using the most recent checkpoint available before that article's month.
  • Real-time feasible: no article uses a topic model trained on future articles.
  • However, the topic representation is no longer a single stable topic space across the full OOS window.

Why this is not a clean look-ahead-bias robustness check

  • 1. Topic identity is not continuous across checkpoints.
    ckpt_2005_topic_17 and ckpt_2012_topic_17 are not necessarily the same economic narrative. This weakens coefficient interpretation, topic importance, and narrative retrieval.
  • 2. Feature dimension expands mechanically.
    With 4 checkpoints and K = 180, the model receives 4 × 180 = 720 topic features plus AR lags — much harder for LASSO / ENet / PCA than the 180-topic full-sample design.
  • 3. Active features are sparse by construction.
    In each month, only the active checkpoint's topic columns are nonzero. The downstream model learns checkpoint-specific feature blocks rather than one stable representation.
  • 4. Checkpoint boundaries create artificial regime shifts.
    When the active checkpoint changes, the feature representation changes discontinuously. The forecasting model may partly capture checkpoint transitions rather than news attention.

Method D is useful as an exploratory real-time-feasible stress test, but should not be treated as the primary leakage robustness check — it changes both the topic representation and the downstream ML feature space. The preferred benchmark is Method A: single-checkpoint frozen topic model.

Hyperparameter Selection
K must be pre-specified — recommendation: K = 180
KSourceRole
80Larsen & Thorsrud (2019); Ellingsen et al. (2022); Larsen et al. (2021)Main

180 ★Bybee et al. (2024) main; Hong et al. (2025) mainMain — recommended
250Hong et al. (2025) robustness; Bybee et al. (2024) discussionRobustness

Our recommendation: K = 180 main, K = 150 and K = 250 as robustness, exactly mirroring Hong et al. (2025). Any K choice must be made before looking at OOS results.

"K = 180 is a sensible choice. A model with fewer topics (e.g., 50) produces mixed topics that contain multiple separate themes. A larger model (e.g., 250 topics) delivers similar interpretability as that for the 180-topic model, but with a number of overly specific topics that capture one-off events. Finally, all models in the neighborhood of K = 180 look very similar in their topic composition." Bybee et al. - 2024 - Business News and Business Cycles (p. 3114)

Ver1 problem (L1.3): K = 2000 was selected by grid-searching {100, 250, 500, 727, 1000, 1500, 2000, 2500, 3000} and picking the K minimizing OOS ENet RMSE. This is hyperparameter selection on the test set and is not defensible. K = 2000 must be replaced regardless of which method we adopt.

Anticipated Reviewer Pushback
Four reviewer clusters and our defenses
Pushback 1
"Bybee-style" — JF / RFS
Likely review text: "The authors estimate LDA on the full corpus… As Bybee et al. (2024, p. 3114) note, θ̂_t is informed in part by term counts that realize after t. The authors should use Online LDA or estimate LDA on a pre-OOS training sample only."

Defense: Following Hong et al. (2025). Cite Ellingsen et al. (2022) and Bybee et al. (2024) that bias is "qualitatively minor." Back with our own Method A robustness numbers at K = 180. Requires actually running Method A at K = 180.
Pushback 2
K-grid leakage — any ML-aware reviewer
Likely review text: "The authors search over a grid of K values and select the K with the lowest OOS RMSE. This is feature/hyperparameter selection on the test set [Kapoor & Narayanan 2023]. The reported gains are not credible."

Defense: K pre-specified at 180 following Bybee et al. (2024) and Hong et al. (2025). K = 150 and K = 250 as robustness. K-sensitivity table shows stability across K = 100–250, demonstrating we are not exploiting a fragile sweet spot.
Pushback 3
Why not as large as Hong's?
Hong et al. (2025) report 12m LDA-ENet vs AR ≈ 0.696. Our Method A at 12m ≈ 0.851. The 0.16 gap looks bad in isolation.

Defense: Hong et al. OOS: 2000–2021 (~258 months, 3 recession episodes — dot-com bust, 2008 GFC, COVID). Our OOS: 2014–2025 (~138 months, COVID-dominated, one calm-period baseline). Hong et al. (2025, p. 217): "outperformance of narrative-based forecasts is mainly documented during recession periods." We have one such episode; they have three.
Pushback 4
Why not oLDA? (sophisticated variant of #1)
Defense: Engineering cost vs marginal benefit. Bybee et al. (2024, p. 3132): gain is "quantitatively minor" and "statistically indistinguishable at long horizons." oLDA burn-in (4 years, Bybee et al. p. 3141) further shortens our already COVID-dominated OOS window. We instead report Method A (following Larsen & Thorsrud 2019; Ellingsen et al. 2022) as our look-ahead-corrected robustness check.
